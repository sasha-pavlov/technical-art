Key takeaways

  1. There are different ways to represent the same data. Learn connections between different representations to solve problems efficiently.
  2. If you don't know of some of the representations of problems, their solutions look like magic.
  3. When faced with a new formula, try graphing it (ex. Desmos).
  4. When faced with trig, try drawing various circles and triangles.
  5. GeoGebra is a good visual tool for representing algebra geometrically and vise versa.

Teaching strats

  1. Live multiple-choice questions: when writing them, make note of what errors would cause incorrect choices. Once answers come in, look at the distribution of responses and comment on likely sources of error. Help students fail forward and thus benefit from having tried answering.

2: Trigonometry fundamentals

Walking around the unit circle

Starting at (1,0)(1,0)(1,0) on the unit circle, with a tether tied to the center of the circle forming an angle θ\thetaθ relative to the start-position, and walking counter-clockwise:

  • sin⁡(θ)\sin(\theta)sin(θ) is the distance from the xxx-axis
  • cos⁡(θ)\cos(\theta)cos(θ) is the distance from the yyy-axis
  • One full circuit is 2π2\pi2π long

Converting between Triangle-trig and Circle-trig

Triangle trigonometry: SOH CAH TOA

  • sin⁡(θ)=o/h\sin(\theta)=o/hsin(θ)=o/h
  • cos⁡(θ)=a/h\cos(\theta)=a/hcos(θ)=a/h
  • tan⁡(θ)=o/a\tan(\theta)=o/atan(θ)=o/a

To instead work with the unit circle, scale the length of each side of the triangle by 1h\frac{1}{h}h1​ so that the hypotenuse is one unit long: h=1h=1h=1. Now,

  • y=sin⁡(θ)=1hoy=\sin(\theta)=\frac{1}{h}oy=sin(θ)=h1​o
  • x=cos⁡(θ)=1hax=\cos(\theta)=\frac{1}{h}ax=cos(θ)=h1​a

Tangent

Projecting new right-angle triangles: If we set the 111-magnitude side to be the adjacent, with the hypotenuse lying atop the x-axis, the tangent is the opposite length. The opposite side is perpendicular to the 111-magnitude adjacent, at the edge of the unit circle, "tangential" to the curve at that point.

Notice that walking around the unit circle, the length of the tangent:

Trigonometric angle sum identities

Instead of memorizing the trig sum identities, think of adding two angles together in terms of rotating a point from (1,0)(1,0)(1,0) twice consecutively, ie. multiplying the complex number representations of the two angles together.

Similarily, think of multiplying an angle by a constant c in terms of rotating a point from (1,0)(1,0)(1,0) c times consecutively.

cis(cα)=cis(α)c[cos⁡(cα)+isin⁡(cα)]=[cos⁡(α)+isin⁡(α)]c cis(c\alpha)=cis(\alpha)^c \\ \lbrack\cos(c\alpha) + i\sin(c\alpha)]= [\cos(\alpha) + i\sin(\alpha)]^c cis(cα)=cis(α)c[cos(cα)+isin(cα)]=[cos(α)+isin(α)]c

Fun facts about imaginary and complex numbers

  • Renee Descartes coined the term imaginary as a derogatory desription of iii, and then we stuck with it as a convention
  • The 90deg⁡90\deg90deg-rotation-like multiplicative effect of iii geometrically justifies the Imaginary dimension being perpendicular to the Real.
  • The i^,j^,k^\hat{i}, \hat{j}, \hat{k}i^,j^​,k^ convention of naming vectors in physics comes from the use of these symbols in quaternions, which are representations of 3D space using imaginary dimensions.
