Key takeaways

  1. There are different ways to represent the same data. Learn connections between different representations to solve problems efficiently.
  2. When faced with a new formula, try graphing it (ex. Desmos).
  3. When faced with trig, try drawing various circles and triangles.

1: The simpler quadratic formula

The product as a difference of squares

Every product of 2 numbers r,sr, sr,s can be represented as a difference of squares.

rs=(m−d)(m+d)=m2−d2 rs = (m-d)(m+d) \\ = m^2 - d^2 rs=(m−d)(m+d)=m2−d2

m,dm,dm,d are the average and standard deviation, respectively.

m=(r+s)/2d=∣m−r∣=∣m−s∣ m = (r+s)/2 \\ d = |m - r| = |m - s| m=(r+s)/2d=∣m−r∣=∣m−s∣

Sum of squares

Using complex numbers, every sum of squares m2+d2m^2 + d^2m2+d2 can be expressed as a difference of squares, and thus factored:

m2+d2=m2−(−1)d2=m2−i2d2=(m+id)(m−id) m^2 + d^2 \\ = m^2 - (-1)d^2 \\ = m^2 - i^2d^2 \\ = (m + id)(m - id) \\ m2+d2=m2−(−1)d2=m2−i2d2=(m+id)(m−id)

2: Trigonometry fundamentals

Walking around the unit circle

Starting at (1,0)(1,0)(1,0) on the unit circle, with a tether tied to the center of the circle forming an angle θ\thetaθ relative to the start-position, and walking counter-clockwise:

  • sin⁡(θ)\sin(\theta)sin(θ) is the distance from the xxx-axis
  • cos⁡(θ)\cos(\theta)cos(θ) is the distance from the yyy-axis
  • One full circuit is 2π2\pi2π long

Converting between Triangle-trig and Circle-trig

Triangle trigonometry: SOH CAH TOA

  • sin⁡(θ)=o/h\sin(\theta)=o/hsin(θ)=o/h
  • cos⁡(θ)=a/h\cos(\theta)=a/hcos(θ)=a/h
  • tan⁡(θ)=o/a\tan(\theta)=o/atan(θ)=o/a

To instead work with the unit circle, scale the length of each side of the triangle by 1h\frac{1}{h}h1​ so that the hypotenuse is one unit long: h=1h=1h=1. Now,

  • y=sin⁡(θ)=1hoy=\sin(\theta)=\frac{1}{h}oy=sin(θ)=h1​o
  • x=cos⁡(θ)=1hax=\cos(\theta)=\frac{1}{h}ax=cos(θ)=h1​a

Tangent

Projecting new right-angle triangles: If we set the 111-magnitude side to be the adjacent, with the hypotenuse lying atop the x-axis, the tangent is the opposite length. The opposite side is perpendicular to the 111-magnitude adjacent, at the edge of the unit circle, "tangential" to the curve at that point.

Notice that walking around the unit circle, the length of the tangent:

  • at (1,0)(1,0)(1,0), tan⁡(0)=0\tan(0) = 0tan(0)=0

Pythagorean theorem

From the side lengths of the "unit-hypotenuse triangle" above,

h=x2+y21=cos⁡2(θ)+sin⁡2(θ) h = x^2 + y^2 \\ 1 = \cos^2(\theta) + \sin^2(\theta) h=x2+y21=cos2(θ)+sin2(θ)

Squared (co)sines

Both sinusoidal and exponential equations (ex. f(x)=2xf(x)=2^xf(x)=2x) have the interesting property of f(2x)≈(f(x))2f(2x) \approx (f(x))^2f(2x)≈(f(x))2.

cos⁡2(θ)=cos⁡(2θ)+12 \cos^2(\theta) = \frac{\cos(2\theta)+1}{2} cos2(θ)=2cos(2θ)+1​

Projecting new right-angle triangles: For any triangle in the unit circle, take the adjacent and use it as the hypotenuse h′h'h′. Then,

cos⁡(θ)=a′/h′=a′/cos⁡(θ)⇒a′=cos⁡2(θ) \cos(\theta) = a'/h' \\ = a'/\cos(\theta) \\ \Rightarrow a'=\cos^2(\theta) cos(θ)=a′/h′=a′/cos(θ)⇒a′=cos2(θ)

Now take the remaining length of the original hypotenuse hhh: o′=h−a′o'=h-a'o′=h−a′. Use the original opposite as the hypotenuse h′′h''h′′.

sin⁡(θ)=o′/h′′=a′/sin⁡(θ)⇒o′=sin⁡2(θ) \sin(\theta) = o'/h'' \\ = a'/\sin(\theta) \\ \Rightarrow o'=\sin^2(\theta) sin(θ)=o′/h′′=a′/sin(θ)⇒o′=sin2(θ)
